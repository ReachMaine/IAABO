MEMO

TO: IAABO BOARD #111 OFFICIALS
FR: Peter Webb, INTERPRETER

The regular season is rapidly coming to a close. At this time of year many games as seen as more meaningful by those involved. Be prepared to meet the challenge.

Maintain a positive attitude. It has been a busy season for most of you. It is easy to get discouraged at times. Don’t let that happen. Every game is a fresh start. The school, coaches, players and spectators are expecting outstanding service from you.

TAKE YOUR TIME! Each and every ruling stop the clock (hold it) then signal properly – DON’T HURRY! This has a very calming effect on the entire gym as well as the crew. If you tend to be too quick try this. It becomes comforting to you; it improves communication for all and can be a confidence builder for you and all who are watching you. This type of officiating enhances the Crew’s professionalism status.

Only the Head Coach may be off the bench/seat.

Traveling — BE SURE that you have identified the pivot foot; remember it by “left, left, left” self talk. Don’t be guilty of ruling a legal move as a travel. There are some awkward looking moves as a player ends a drive or dribble and continues to move feet while attempting to pass. Usually the movement is legal. Often I observe it being ruled illegal. A pivot foot is placed on the floor. Next the stopping foot is placed on the floor.
Next the pivot foot is lifted off of the floor and the leg/foot is then reaching out, in a follow through move and the player passes or attempts a try for goal. This is a legal as the pivot foot is not returned to the floor prior to the ball being released. Think hard —
TO RULE THIS A TRAVEL TAKES THE BALL AWAY FROM THE TEAM WHO LEGALLY HAS IT AND AWARDS THE TEAM WHO SHOULD STILL BE ON DEFENSE. If you were the player or coach would you be upset? This happens.

Traveling/Pivot Foot as regards the post player. — Basically the same as above. A post player maintains a pivot foot (identify it) while moving the non-pivot foot first to the left (in a cross over step) then moves the non-pivot foot back to the right, places it on the floor then pushes off with it while reaching out with the pivot foot and releases the ball on a try or pass prior to replacing the old pivot on the floor. Legal. It may cover a lot of space but is not a violation. In this case an official not caring enough to identify the pivot foot may take away a legal two points from Team A, deprive them of both the points and the ball then give the ball to Team B. What an effect on the game and feelings players, coaches and fans.

The Whistle — There are officials who think other officials “blow the whistle too much”. Those who think that way should examine their own work and ask do I “blow the whistle enough?”
Usually the “calls” that we make with a whistle don’t hurt the game and most people can live with it. However, the “calls” that we make and don’t blow the whistle do hurt/effect the game. It has been known for years that coaches, given a choice, would prefer more whistles as opposed to not enough whistles. In general, the official who tends to “let them play” is putting him/her self into the game. That creates unfairness and that is as bad as it gets. If that is your thought process, it is BAD – change.

Contact — The rules are not anti an aggressive defense or anti screening offenses. However, the rules do not permit illegal contact. Bumping the dribbler in the backcourt, bumping the cutters going through and around the lane area are FOULS. Placing a hand(s)/arms on a dribbler in the backcourt, the frontcourt or on a drive to the basket is a FOUL. Offensive players using forearms and displacing the defender or pushing off while screening is a FOUL.

Contact, especially at the end of the game, should be monitored and ruled the same as it is ruled all game long. There is LEGAL CONTACT there is ILLEGAL CONTACT.

Example, Team A has scored. B-1’s throw in goes to B-2. B-2 gets by defender A-2.
Now A-2 is behind B-2. While both are progressing toward the division line, A-2 behind B-2, A-2 reaches out and contacts B-2. Is that contact? YES. Is that illegal contact? NO. Should a foul be ruled? NO. The contact is INCIDENTAL (4.27). The contact did not affect the play of B-2. That player should still be dribbling and helping the team in an offensive effort. Why is such a situation ruled a foul?

Ball watching — Check yourself. If you are a ball watcher you are hurting the crew and not serving the game very well.

Officials talking too much. — The majority of the concerns/complaints that are received by the Interpreter and the Commissioner involve “talking officials”. Complaints regarding officials’ rulings seldom get beyond the non-liking stage of the coach or fan. Officials can eliminate the “talking” problem by not talking. One can not improve on silence. Silence can not be quoted. Let your whistle of no whistle do the talking. When the official talks – misstatements take place; misunderstanding takes place; embarrassment takes place; feelings are hurt; defensive attitudes take place; response take place; etc. The result often is that officials hurt themselves and the overall appraisals of all officials suffer.

Gossip – Officials talking about schools, coaches, players, fans, fellow officials in a careless manner serves no good purpose. If you were “down” would you want someone gossiping about you? A technical foul, an ejection, misbehaving fans are just that. They either have been penalized and/or dealt with otherwise. It is just a part of the event. When the game is over, those things are in the past. Report them to the proper source and cast the issue aside. No one wants to hear of their misbehavior being broadcast.

Keep up the overall very good officiating and overall service that you provide. Do some self evaluation and together make these last two weeks the very best that we can. Be an outstanding partner. Be prepared, have a thorough pre-game conference. Be alert from the time you arrive on the court. Don’t be caught by surprise. Attend to business. Shine professionally.

Accentuate the Positive – Eliminate the Negative. Work hard, take things seriously and realize as much fun as possible.

The Interpreter and the Commissioner are supporters of you and your work.

Thank you for listening
