2009-10 Coordinator of Interpreters’ Mid-Season Memo

Touching Base With Points of Emphasis
Hi-Lite Review & Check Points

Closely Guarded – The rule applies anywhere in the frontcourt when there is player control…legal guarding position, within six feet, when guarding a player holding or dribbling the ball… no particular stance required, doesn’t require aggressive/intense play by the defender…multiple defenders may be involved provided each maintains the six foot requirement when the “switch” occurs…the count ends and a new count begins when the offensive player ends the dribble and holds the ball or ends the holding and begins a dribble…the rule and count is discontinued when the defender is “beaten” (offensive player gets head and shoulders past the front plane of the defender) or when the distance becomes greater than six feet…if you don’t know if it is six feet, how do you know when it is not six feet?...care about recognizing the situation and effort of the defense, apply the rule and the count…it matters !

Guarding and Block/Charge – Defender obtains legal guarding position (initially, both feet on the floor and facing the opponent)…then may maintain the position which has been obtained until “beaten” ( the offensive player gets head and shoulders past the defender’s front plane…time & distance are factors in obtaining legal guarding position when the offensive player is moving without the ball…time & distance are NOT factors when the offensive player is stationery with or without the ball or moving with the ball…if the offensive player is airborne prior to the defender obtaining legal guarding position, the offensive player must allow the opponent landing space…the defender has a rule given right to move the feet, to jump and does not need to be still/set upon contact.. the defender may turn, etc. to absorb the force of contact.

Free Throw Administration – Care about the involved rules…there are two teams involved…carelessness on the official’s part may favor one team…the plane of the free throw lane line can’t be broken, with a foot, until the ball is at the basket or backboard…potential disconcertion must be monitored…lane space players can not leave the 3’ x 3’ lane space in any direction, prior to the ball until the ball is at the basket or backboard…Trail or Center official shall not close toward the endline prior to the ball reaching the basket or backboard…proper coverage and enforcement of rules will nearly eliminate the potential for rough play.

Three-Seconds – Be a good three-second official…care about monitoring free throw lane play…the lane’s proximity to the basket and high percentage rate of scoring from that area makes the coverage and attention vital to fair play…instances of 4 to 9 seconds are not uncommon…Lead official ball watching and neglecting the free throw lane coverage when the ball is outside of his/her PCA is not fair to the defending team…Trail and/or Center must aid in free throw lane coverage, when the ball is not in their PCA, by expanding their FOV cone.

Traveling – Be focused/concentrate, locate and maintain (“left, left”) coverage of the pivot foot…watch the player receive the pass, secure the rebound, end the dribble, begin the 3-point attempt, attempt a jump stop, attempting a behind the back dribble, attempting a “spin” move, preparing to dunk…illegal use of the pivot foot and/or ending a dribble and beginning another dribble is a major advantage to the offense and a major disadvantage to the defense.

Continuous Motion – It is a rule given right of an offensive player, with the ball, to try for a goal anytime anyhow and anyplace on the court, provided he/she does so legally… the player need only to have “merely” begun foot, body, arm movement which habitually are associated with trying for a goal…the “shooter” or a teammate being fouled does not cause the ball to become dead…the official’s whistle and stop the clock signal simply stops the clock and indicates a foul, the ball remains “live”…a foul committed by a teammate of the “shooter”, prior to the try being released, does cause the ball to become dead and therefore ends the try…the “shooter’s” foot/feet being in touch with the floor when the foul by the defense occurs, does not end the try for goal, Continuous Motion prevails. Officials must be careful to not penalize a player who is simply carrying out her/his rule given right to try for goal…the culprit in the situation is the defender, penalize the appropriate player.

Flopping – Faking Being Fouled
Acting As If Charged Rule 10.3.6 (f)

The defensive player or screener acting as though he/she has been charged or pushed by an opponent, when in fact he/she has not been charged or pushed very definitely has an adverse effect upon the game. Without a doubt the practice is detrimental to the best interest of the game. The “actor” wants to create the false impression that he/she has been fouled when there has been no contact or it there was contact it was incidental/legal (4.27). The “acting” guard or screener falls to the floor as though he/she was placed there by the force of the opponent. Through this action the player makes it appear that the opponent ought to be penalized with a foul. It also incites the spectators. Flopping (10.3.6 (f), as though being fouled, is an unsporting act. Such conduct must be penalized with a technical foul. Enforce the rule and eliminate the “act.” 

 Intentional Foul

Far too often officials do not rule a foul as intentional when the act clearly meets the criteria of an intentional foul. The intentional foul has developed into an official’s personal interpretation and misapplication of the rule. The defensive team is given an unwarranted advantage when an intentional foul is not ruled when committed. Intentional fouls may occur at anytime during the game. The foul should be ruled intentional regardless of time and score.

Some things to be aware of/look for are:

  1. Any contact which is not a legitimate attempt to play the ball.

  2. Grabbing a player’s arm or body without initially attempting to play or gain control of the ball.

  3. Any holding, pushing or grabbing a player away from the ball.

  4. Grabbing a player’s uniform.

  5. Wrapping arm(s) around a player.

  6. Pushing/grapping a player from behind before or the act of shooting begins or during the act of shooting (example, an apparent easy goal ).

  7. An obvious attempt to stop the clock or neutralize the opponent’s obvious advantageous position by forcing the opponent into a designated spot throw-in or free throw situation.

Toss

By rule, the toss must be higher than either player can jump…The ball is often contacted on its way up, it is a violation and should be ruled as such…Observers note more poor tosses than accurate tosses and no ruling being made requiring a re-toss…The tossing official may rule that a re-toss is needed.

Mid-Season Notes - Additional

Traveling – A Point of Emphasis (NFHS & IAABO)
It is good to observe this POE receiving attention. However, it is not a traveling violation because we THINK it is. It is only a traveling violation because we KNOW it is. It is not serving the rule and purpose to “split hairs.” Again, the emphasis is attending to and KNOWING as opposed to simply THINKING or guessing regarding the use of the pivot foot. The pivot foot must be identified, then monitored for legal or illegal use of the pivot foot. Reminder, the use of the pivot foot is considerably different when a player is moving while passing or trying for goal than it is when beginning a dribble. There can be very unorthodox movement prior to a pass or try for goal and the movement be legal.

Hand checking, bumping, pushing, holding and in general aggressive defensive and offensive contact not being ruled fouls. Such contact affects ball handlers, cutters, screeners, rebounders, post players, individual and team styles of play – both defensive and offensive players are affected and must be closely monitored. The official does not have the choice of whether the contact matters to him/her. What matters is, it does matter to the players and teams because it affects what they want and need to accomplish.

Loose ball – monitor the contact closely. Bumps, etc. can affect the end result – it matters.

Potential held ball/jump ball situations – both players must be controlling the ball or a hand must be so firmly on the ball that control cannot be obtained without undue roughness in order to rule a held ball.

Player out of bounds (thrower; due to momentum; falls out of bounds; etc).The player returns to inbounds status when he/she is not in touch with out-of-bounds. One foot in-bounds is sufficient. Other than the thrower of the throw-in, a player returning from out-of-bounds may be the first to touch/catch the ball in-bounds. In and of itself, returning to in-bounds status and being the first to touch or gather in the ball is not a violation.

Substitutions – Do not hold up the game to admit substitutes. Seeing a team member move toward the table is to be ignored. Substitutes must report and the horn must sound prior to the official being ready to administer the throw-in or free throw. Officials are too lax with this item.

As center official, don’t retreat (transition) too soon, remain and read the coverage needs.

When reporting fouls, time-outs, warnings - hustle to and from the reporting area; don’t walk & talk. Reminder, do not overlook the “freezing field of vision” foul coverage mechanic.

Primarily, the Team Control Signal means there will be no free throws as the foul was committed by a player, of the offensive team, who was not in control of the ball – don’t get excited and make it a “punch”. It is not a specific type of foul. The type of foul is hold, push, etc.

Palming & Carrying – needs attention and enforcement. Observations find there are at least as many palming or carrying violations going unattended to as there are traveling violations unattended to. Often the ball handler plays with the ball like it is attached to a yo-yo. “Cupping” (hand under the ball holding it/catching it and throwing a short bounce pass toward her/his other hand, not dribbling the ball, then “cupping” and throwing it back to the other hand. After two or three “palming”/ending of the dribble, the player then easily beats the defender.

Officials make mistakes. Realize it and understand it. Our mistakes affect players and teams and they matter to coaches. It is not only players and coaches who make mistakes. Have a passion and professional pride – prepare thoroughly to limit your mistakes.

Communication with coaches – don’t hold up the game (allowance for rare exception); primarily be a listener; one can’t improve upon silence; be composed, be courteous; be professional; never personalize a response; don’t argue; consider bringing both coaches together when you do take time to listen or respond to a coach; never react with “shut up”, “your wrong”, etc. Don’t display, verbally or physically, an “attitude.” What happens in the gym, stays in the gym. If a player or coach is penalized for misbehavior, don’t double tax them by gossiping with fellow officials about the person or incident.

Officials receiving both high school and college assignments – respect both rules, mechanics and signals codes. At the high school level, only the high school rules, mechanics and signals are acceptable. Be professional and care, honor the level which you are serving. Observations noted – closing on the release of the free throw; not getting to the reporting area; not signaling the clock to stop violation rulings; no at the site of the foul signals & information (hit & run officiating); too much conversation with coaches when the ball is in play; no stop the clock/time-out signal when the time-out is granted; lax closely guarded application.

Food For Thought and Review Items

The game is to be played according to the rules, NOT according to officials and coaches individual philosophies.

When the official finds the rules and accepts them, he/she will find his/her purpose within the game.

Can officials make accurate rulings on rules they don’t know?... Do the coaches, players, fans and media have the right to assume that officials know the rules?

Officiating is not an Art.., Don’t try to make it an Art… Rather, officiating is a Science.

The vast majority of rulings made during a game do not require a whistle and penalty as the action is legal. Some rulings, on the average-60 per game, are accompanied by a whistle and are penalized because the action is illegal…The point is, we are making rulings throughout the game – thousands of our rulings do not require a whistle and penalty.

Rebounding play – See the entire play, the beginning, development and the end…Be certain that what appears to be “on the back” is not being “put on the back/backing out” by the inside rebounder.

It is not the role of an official to control or manage the game…The official’s role is to monitor the play, apply the rule and arrive at a ruling of legal or illegal.

Don’t let praise or criticism get to you…It is a weakness to get caught up in either one.

Don’t personalize criticism…Be a listener… Learn to listen.

You can’t officiate when upset or angry with self or others…It is difficult to make accurate decisions when angry.

No one is in charge of your officiating happiness but you.

Treat players, coaches and fellow officials the way you would like to be treated…Respect the game and the participants at all times.

Realize that the game and the opportunity to play if means much to the participants for many and various reasons.

Don’t be caught by surprise…Be alert/Be ready/Be prepared at all times, including during dead ball time.

Casual application of rules can favor a style of play or harm a style of play.

What is allowed/condoned is encouraged to continue…Tolerance favors compliance or non-compliance?...We reap what we sow—what we don’t enforce, we encourage!

Enforce the rules, players and coaches will adjust.

Enforce the rules with no regard to time and score.

Be thorough with instruction to scorer and timer.

Be thorough with informing the table personnel and Head Coaches when informing them of extra period procedures (jump ball, direction of play, four minutes, time-outs, etc.).

Help situations…May be asked for…May be offered…Be clear when sharing information don’t cause confusion.

During pre-game on the court, use the time wisely…It is a vital part of preparation time (officials; warm-up time)… Observe the use of pivot foot (3-point attempts, “spin” moves, post players practicing, etc).

Make eye contact with partner every time a whistle is sounded or heard.

Be thorough with all at the site of foul information…Be thorough with all at the site of violation information.

At times, it is heard about signals “It doesn’t matter.”… If “it doesn’t matter”, do what is expected - do it right?...A majority of the signals are required By Rule.

Signals are the language of the game…Signals are vital to communications with all involved…Additionally, taking the time to signal/communicate, as the game expects, can serve as a calming/pacing /settling affect upon a hectic, noisy and emotional setting.

The greatest virtues in officiating are composure and wisdom.

Always demonstrate professionalism, composure, confidence, understanding, dignity, and integrity.

Always have a thorough pre-game conference...Use of IAABO pre-game card is encouraged.

Post game…Take time to review, comment, share feedback…Individual & Crew appraisal, questions, recommendations… We are all in this together…Be the best that we can be.

Consider requesting a DVD of the game, do so a couple of times per season…Deal with the Athletic Administrator, not the coach…Offer to pay for it.

Have you received a copy of IAABO’s Early Season Memo? If not, consider asking your Interpreter for a copy…Best wishes for an enjoyable and successful remainder of the season.
